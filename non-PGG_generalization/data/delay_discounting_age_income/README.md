# Age-Related Differences in Delay Discounting: Income Matters

## Citation
Wan, H., Myerson, J., Green, L., Strube, M. J., & Hale, S. (2024). Age-related differences in delay discounting: Income matters. *Psychology and Aging*, 39(6), 632-643.

## Source
- **OSF** (data): https://osf.io/um68t/
- **GitHub** (replication code): https://github.com/haoranmattwan/age-income-discounting-2024
- **DOI**: https://doi.org/10.1037/pag0000818

## Recruitment
- **Platform**: Prolific
- **Country**: US-based (screened for US IP address)
- **Collection year**: 2022
- **Stratification**: 4 strata defined by age x income:
  - Ages 35-50 vs 65-80
  - Annual household income <$50k vs >$80k
- **Sample**: n=423 recruited, n=359 analyzed (after exclusions for out-of-category, attention checks, gender filtering for binary comparison)

## Payment Structure (Non-IC)
Prolific participation pay (amount not reported). Discounting tasks use **hypothetical monetary amounts**---choices between immediate and delayed money are not paid out. Decisions are purely hypothetical.

## Design
- **Monetary Choice Questionnaire (MCQ)**: 27 binary choices (3 sets of 9) between smaller-sooner and larger-later amounts
- **Adjusting-Amount procedure**: Multiple delays and amounts ($30, $80, $500; several delays with randomization/counterbalancing)
- **Additional measures**: Hospital Anxiety and Depression Scale (HADS)
- **Multiplayer**: No

## Key Variables
- **Behavioral**: MCQ delayed-reward choices (proportion choosing delayed), adjusting-amount implied subjective values/discounting curves
- **Stratification**: Age group (35-50 vs 65-80), income group (<$50k vs >$80k)
- **Covariates**: HADS psychological distress scores
- **Key finding**: Younger, lower-income adults discount more steeply than older, lower-income adults; no age difference among higher-income groups

## Demographics
Age group, gender, education ("college or above"), race/ethnicity ("not Hispanic or Latino", "White") within each group.

## Ethics

## Suitability for Twin-Profile Prediction
High suitability if Twin profiles include time preference proxies, impulsivity/conscientiousness, SES features (income/education), and mental health indicators. The explicit age x income stratification creates plausible subgroups where Twin features may add predictive value unevenly (e.g., larger uplift among lower-income younger adults).
